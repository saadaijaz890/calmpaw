Dog Anxiety Symptoms: 15 Signs You Should Know

Some anxiety signs are obvious. Others — lip licking, excessive yawning, whale eye — are easy to miss. Here's every symptom to watch for, and what it means.

🔬 Vet-reviewed📅 Updated 2026⌛ 10 min read

Why Recognizing Anxiety Matters

Anxiety that goes unrecognized becomes anxiety that gets worse. Dogs can't tell you they're stressed — but they communicate it constantly through body language. The earlier you catch the signs, the easier it is to intervene before anxiety becomes ingrained behavior.

Obvious Signs of Dog Anxiety

🗣️

Excessive barking or howling

Especially when left alone, during storms, or in new environments

🚪

Destructive behavior

Chewing furniture, doors, or belongings — particularly near exits

🚽

Indoor accidents

House-trained dog suddenly eliminating indoors — often separation anxiety

🏃

Pacing and restlessness

Repetitive movement, inability to settle — especially before triggers

😰

Shaking or trembling

Visible trembling during storms, at the vet, or around strangers

🙈

Hiding

Retreating under beds, behind furniture, into closets during stressors

😤

Excessive panting

Heavy panting with no physical exertion or heat — a stress response

💦

Drooling

Excessive salivation in anticipation of stressors (car trips, grooming)

Subtle Signs Most Owners Miss

These are calming signals — dog body language that says "I'm stressed." Trainers read these constantly. Most owners don't notice them until the dog escalates to obvious behaviors.

1

Lip licking (when not eating)

A quick tongue flick over the nose or lips. One of the earliest stress signals. Common at the vet, with strangers, or during training when the dog is confused.

2

Yawning (out of context)

A slow, wide yawn when the dog isn't tired. Dogs yawn to self-soothe and to signal discomfort to others. If your dog yawns every time you hug them, that's a stress signal.

3

Whale eye

When a dog turns their head away but keeps their eyes on you — showing the whites of their eyes in a half-moon shape. Means they're uncomfortable and don't want to engage.

4

Ears pinned back

Flattened ears against the skull. Mild stress signal often combined with tail tucking. Different from relaxed ears that hang naturally.

5

Tail tucked below the body

Classic fear/stress indicator. Even a slight tuck matters. High, stiff tail = arousal/alertness; low or tucked = fear or stress.

6

Turning away or freezing

A dog that suddenly goes still or turns their whole body away from something is communicating "I'm not okay with this." Freezing before a bite is common — it's a warning sign.

7

Hypervigilance

Constantly scanning the environment, unable to focus, startling easily at normal sounds. Looks like the dog is always "on guard." Common in rescue dogs and those with PTSD-like histories.

Types of Dog Anxiety by Symptom Pattern

Symptom PatternLikely Anxiety Type
Only when alone; destructive near exitsSeparation anxiety
Shaking/hiding during storms or fireworksNoise phobia
Reactive on leash, lunging at dogs/peopleFear-based leash reactivity
Pacing/panting at night, especially in seniorsCognitive dysfunction or pain anxiety
Anxious in all new environments or with strangersGeneralized anxiety disorder
Compulsive licking, tail chasing, spinningOCD/compulsive disorder (anxiety-driven)

When Anxiety Becomes an Emergency

See a vet immediately if: Your dog is causing self-injury (broken teeth from crate bars, bleeding paws), refusing to eat for more than 48 hours, having panic attacks lasting over 30 minutes, or showing sudden aggression when previously gentle. These indicate severe anxiety that needs professional intervention.

What to Do Next

Once you've identified anxiety in your dog, the next step is figuring out the type and severity. Mild anxiety (occasional lip licking, mild panting) can often be managed with calming chews and environmental management. Moderate to severe anxiety — especially if it involves any physical symptoms or lasts hours — warrants a vet visit and potentially a medication consultation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can anxiety make a dog sick?

Yes. Chronic stress suppresses the immune system, can cause GI issues (diarrhea, vomiting), and increases cortisol which affects long-term health. Untreated severe anxiety should always be addressed.

How do I know if my dog has anxiety or a medical problem?

Many anxiety symptoms overlap with medical issues — panting can mean pain, pacing can mean neurological problems, house accidents can mean UTI. If symptoms are new or sudden, rule out medical causes first with a vet checkup.

Do dogs grow out of anxiety?

Rarely without intervention. Puppies with anxiety tend to become adult dogs with anxiety unless the triggers are addressed during socialization. Early intervention is always better.
