Dog Anxiety at Night: 7 Causes and How to Fix Each

nighttime anxiety is exhausting for everyone. Here are the 7 most common causes — with targeted fixes for each — so you and your dog can actually sleep.

🔬 Vet-reviewed📅 Updated 2026⌛ 8 min read

Why Nighttime Anxiety Happens

Nighttime removes all the daytime distractions — activity, stimulation, your presence and movement. Whatever anxiety was manageable during the day becomes impossible to ignore at night. The quiet also amplifies sounds that trigger noise-sensitive dogs. Understanding the root cause is essential because the fix is completely different depending on why it's happening.

7 Causes and Their Fixes

1. Separation Anxiety

Signs: Whining, pacing, scratching at the bedroom door, calm only when in the same room as you.

Fix: Move your dog's bed next to yours temporarily — this often resolves nighttime whining immediately. Long-term, run an Adaptil diffuser in the bedroom and work on independence training during the day. Feeding a calming chew 60 minutes before bed helps.

2. Canine Cognitive Dysfunction (Senior Dogs)

Signs: Pacing, confusion, vocalizing at night but not during the day, staring at walls, getting "stuck" in corners. Most common in dogs over 10.

Fix: Melatonin (0.1 mg/kg) 30 min before bed helps reset the circadian rhythm. Senilife supplement daily supports cognitive function. For significant CCD, ask your vet about Anipryl (selegiline) — the only FDA-approved drug for canine cognitive dysfunction.

3. Pain (Arthritis or Other)

Signs: Difficulty settling, frequently changing position, reluctance to lie on one side, getting up and lying down repeatedly. Often mistaken for anxiety.

Fix: Orthopedic memory foam bed. Ramps for furniture access. Vet evaluation for NSAIDs or gabapentin — treating the pain eliminates the nighttime restlessness. Don't manage pain-driven anxiety with calming products alone.

4. Noise Sensitivity

Signs: Anxiety correlates with specific sounds — traffic, neighbors, other animals. Gets worse during storms or on weekends.

Fix: White noise machine (brown noise setting works best for dogs). Close windows, use blackout curtains to reduce light triggers. Adaptil collar worn at night. Melatonin for storm-heavy nights.

5. New Environment or Schedule Change

Signs: Sudden onset after a move, new baby, new pet, or major routine change. Was fine before the change.

Fix: Temporary — usually resolves in 2–4 weeks with consistent routine. Adaptil diffuser accelerates adjustment. Maintain the same bedtime routine every night during the transition period.

6. Insufficient Daytime Exercise

Signs: Pacing and restlessness, particularly in young dogs and high-energy breeds. Calm during active periods, anxious when confined at night.

Fix: Increase afternoon exercise (not within 2 hours of bedtime). Add a 15-minute scent work session before bed — mental fatigue is more effective than physical exhaustion for settling anxious dogs.

7. Medical Issues (UTI, GI, Hyperthyroidism)

Signs: Nighttime restlessness accompanied by increased urination, vomiting, diarrhea, or sudden onset with no behavioral explanation.

Fix: vet visit first. Medical causes of nighttime anxiety must be ruled out before treating behaviorally. A UTI that causes nighttime discomfort won't respond to calming supplements.

Nighttime Calming Protocol

1

60 min before bed

Give calming chew (VetriScience Composure) or melatonin (0.1 mg/kg). Plug in Adaptil diffuser in bedroom.

2

30 min before bed

Calm, low-energy activity — short leash walk or gentle play. Avoid high-arousal activities within 2 hours of bed.

3

Bedtime setup

Orthopedic bed in a consistent location. White noise running. Bedroom door open or dog sleeping nearby if separation is the trigger.

4

If they wake at night

Don't make it exciting — quiet reassurance, guide back to bed. No play, no feeding. Consistent response teaches the dog the night routine.

FAQs

Should my dog sleep in my bedroom?

If separation is driving nighttime anxiety, yes — sleeping in the same room (on their own bed) often resolves it immediately. This doesn't worsen separation anxiety during the day and is well-supported by behavioral research.

My senior dog just started pacing at night — is it dementia?

Possibly. Sudden onset nighttime pacing in a dog over 9–10 is commonly CCD, but pain is equally common and often overlooked. A vet visit to check both is the right first step before starting any supplement protocol.

Can anxiety cause a dog to not sleep at night?

Yes. Cortisol disrupts normal sleep architecture in dogs just as it does in humans. Chronically elevated nighttime cortisol (from anxiety or Cushing's disease) prevents deep sleep and causes frequent waking.
