Dog Panting at Night: 8 Causes and What to Do

Nighttime panting keeps both you and your dog awake — and it always means something. Here are the 8 most common causes, from anxiety to pain to Cushing's disease, and what to do about each.

🔬 Vet-reviewed📅 Updated 2026⌛ 10 min read

Is Nighttime Panting Normal?

Occasional panting after exercise or on a hot night is normal. But if your dog is panting regularly at night in a cool room with no obvious cause, it's a signal — usually of discomfort, anxiety, or a medical condition. Nighttime specifically is important: the house is quiet, there are fewer distractions, and your dog has nothing to do but feel whatever they're feeling.

8 Causes of Nighttime Panting in Dogs

1

Anxiety / Generalized Stress

The most common cause in otherwise healthy dogs. Anxiety panting typically comes with pacing, restlessness, and inability to settle. May be triggered by sounds, schedule changes, or a new family member. Start with calming chews given 60 min before bed plus a pheromone diffuser in the bedroom.

2

Pain (Most Common in Seniors)

Arthritis, dental pain, or internal discomfort often worsens at night when the dog lies still. Pain panting is often accompanied by position-shifting, reluctance to lie down, or guarding a body part. See your vet — NSAIDs or gabapentin can dramatically improve quality of life.

3

Canine Cognitive Dysfunction (CCD)

Dog dementia. Affects up to 28% of dogs aged 11–12 and 68% of dogs over 15. Signs: panting, pacing at night, confusion, staring at walls, disrupted sleep cycle. There's no cure but melatonin, Senilife supplements, and prescription Anipryl (selegiline) can help.

4

Cushing's Disease (Hyperadrenocorticism)

Overproduction of cortisol. Classic signs: pot belly, hair loss, increased thirst/urination, and heavy panting — often at night. Diagnosable with a simple blood test. Treatable with trilostane or mitotane.

5

Medication Side Effects

Steroids (prednisone) are a very common cause of panting in dogs. If your dog recently started a new medication and now pants at night, that's likely the cause. Never stop steroids abruptly — discuss with your vet about tapering.

6

Heart or Respiratory Issues

Panting can be compensatory breathing when the heart or lungs aren't working efficiently. Look for exercise intolerance, bluish gums, or a cough. Requires immediate veterinary evaluation.

7

Overheating

Even in winter, sleeping near a heating vent or under heavy blankets can cause a dog to overheat. Flat-faced breeds (Pugs, French Bulldogs, Bulldogs) are especially vulnerable. Ensure the sleeping area is well-ventilated and below 70°F.

8

Hormonal Changes or Pregnancy

Intact females may pant before and during heat cycles. Pregnant dogs pant in the days before labor. If your dog is intact and panting at night, a vet check is warranted.

When to See a Vet Immediately

Emergency signs alongside panting: Blue or white gums, labored breathing, distended belly, collapse, or panting that started suddenly after eating (potential bloat/GDV). These are life-threatening emergencies — go to an emergency vet now.

Frequently Asked Questions

My senior dog just started panting at night — is it dementia?

Possibly. Canine cognitive dysfunction (CCD) is the dog equivalent of Alzheimer's and is very common in dogs over 10. Other signs include confusion, getting "stuck" in corners, and waking at night. A vet checkup can assess and there are treatments that help.

Should I let my dog sleep in my bed if they're anxious at night?

This is personal preference. Co-sleeping doesn't cause or worsen anxiety — it can actually help anxious dogs feel more secure. If it disrupts your sleep enough to be a problem, a dog bed placed right next to yours is a good middle ground.

Can exercise help with nighttime panting?

Yes for anxiety-driven panting. A good 45–60 minute walk or play session in the late afternoon significantly reduces nighttime restlessness. Avoid vigorous exercise within 2 hours of bedtime though — it can have the opposite effect.
