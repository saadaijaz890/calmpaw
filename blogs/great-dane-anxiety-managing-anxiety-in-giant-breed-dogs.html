Why Great Danes Are Prone to Anxiety

Great Danes are often called "gentle giants" for good reason. Despite their imposing size — standing up to 32 inches at the shoulder and weighing 140–175 pounds — these dogs are deeply sensitive, people-oriented, and emotionally attuned to their families. That same sensitivity, however, makes them especially vulnerable to anxiety disorders.

Unlike smaller breeds that might hide their stress, a Great Dane's anxiety is impossible to ignore. A frightened Dane can knock over furniture, damage door frames, and even injure themselves in a panic. Understanding why giant breeds develop anxiety is the first step toward helping them feel safe.

Great Danes were originally bred as boar hunters and later as estate guardians. They are hardwired to bond closely with their people and to be alert to their environment. When that bond is disrupted — by isolation, loud noises, or unfamiliar situations — their natural vigilance can spiral into full-blown anxiety.

Common contributing factors include:

  • Separation sensitivity — Danes are prone to separation anxiety because they form intense attachments to their owners. Being left alone for long hours can trigger panic.
  • Fear of loud noises — Thunderstorms, fireworks, and construction sounds are common triggers. Their size doesn't protect them from sound sensitivity.
  • Lack of early socialization — Without proper exposure to people, places, and other animals during puppyhood, Danes can become fearful and reactive as adults.
  • Boredom and under-exercise — A bored Great Dane is an anxious Great Dane. These dogs need both physical activity and mental stimulation to stay balanced.
  • Genetic predisposition — Some lines of Great Danes are simply more anxious than others. Responsible breeding matters.

Every dog is an individual, but certain triggers appear consistently in anxious Great Danes. Recognizing your dog's specific triggers is essential for creating an effective management plan.

Symptoms of Anxiety in Great Danes

Anxiety in a Great Dane can look different than in a smaller dog. Because of their size, their stress signals are often more physical and more disruptive. Here are the most common signs to watch for:

  • Tail tucking and cowering — A tucked tail is one of the earliest signs of fear. Your Dane may also try to make themselves smaller by crouching or pressing against walls.
  • Excessive panting and drooling — Even in cool weather, an anxious Dane may pant heavily and drool more than usual.
  • Pacing and restlessness — Unable to settle, your dog may walk in circles, pace back and forth, or constantly shift positions.
  • Destructive behavior — Chewing door frames, scratching at walls, and destroying bedding are common in Danes with separation anxiety.
  • Barking or whining — A distressed Dane may vocalize persistently, especially when left alone.
  • Loss of appetite — Some anxious dogs refuse food or treats, even high-value ones.
  • Escape attempts — In severe cases, a panicked Dane may try to break through windows, doors, or fences.

If you notice any of these symptoms repeatedly, it's time to take action. Anxiety doesn't go away on its own — and in giant breeds, it can escalate quickly.

How to Manage Anxiety in Great Danes

Managing anxiety in a Great Dane requires a multi-faceted approach. There is no single pill or product that will fix the problem. Instead, think of it as building a toolkit of strategies that work together.

1. Exercise and Mental Stimulation

A tired Dane is a calmer Dane. But "tired" doesn't just mean physically exhausted — it also means mentally satisfied. Great Danes need both.

Physical exercise: Aim for at least 60 minutes of activity daily, split into two or three sessions. This can include walks, gentle jogging, or playtime in a securely fenced yard. Avoid high-impact exercise like intense fetch or hard running on pavement until your dog is fully grown (around 18–24 months) to protect their joints.

Mental stimulation: Puzzle toys, nose work games, and basic obedience training are excellent outlets. Even 10–15 minutes of focused training can tire a Dane more than a long walk. Consider using a snuffle mat or a treat-dispensing toy during times when you need your dog to settle.

2. Training and Socialization

Training is not just about teaching commands — it's about building confidence. A dog who knows what to expect is a dog who feels safe.

  • Positive reinforcement only — Harsh corrections can worsen anxiety. Reward calm behavior with treats, praise, or gentle petting.
  • Desensitization and counter-conditioning — Gradually expose your dog to their triggers at a low intensity while pairing the experience with something positive (like high-value treats). Over time, this can change their emotional response.
  • Socialization — Even adult Danes can benefit from controlled, positive exposure to new people, places, and calm dogs. Go at your dog's pace and never force interactions.

For a deeper dive into training techniques, check out our comprehensive anxiety guides.

3. Create a Safe Space

Great Danes need a place where they can retreat when they feel overwhelmed. This could be a crate (large enough for them to stand and turn around), a quiet corner with a bed, or even a designated room.

Make the space comfortable with soft bedding, a familiar blanket, and perhaps a piece of your clothing that carries your scent. Some dogs also find comfort in having a "den-like" environment — you can drape a lightweight cover over a crate to create a sense of security.

Introduce the safe space gradually and always associate it with positive experiences. Never use it as punishment.

When to See a Vet

If your Great Dane's anxiety is severe — causing self-injury, destructive behavior, or a complete refusal to eat — it's time to involve your veterinarian. Anxiety is a medical condition, and it deserves the same seriousness as any physical illness.

Your vet may recommend:

  • Prescription medications such as fluoxetine (Prozac) or trazodone for moderate to severe cases.
  • Referral to a veterinary behaviorist for complex anxiety disorders.
  • Blood work to rule out underlying medical conditions that could mimic or worsen anxiety (e.g., thyroid issues, pain).

Never give your dog over-the-counter human medications or supplements without veterinary guidance. Dosages for giant breeds are different than for smaller dogs, and some ingredients can be toxic.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Great Danes naturally anxious dogs?

No, Great Danes are not inherently anxious, but they are a sensitive breed that bonds deeply with their families. This sensitivity, combined with their size and guardian heritage, makes them more prone to developing anxiety — especially separation anxiety and noise phobias — if their needs for companionship, exercise, and routine are not met.

Can a Great Dane's anxiety be cured?

While there is no "cure" for anxiety in the way there is for an infection, most cases can be managed very effectively with the right combination of training, environmental changes, and sometimes medication or calming products. Many anxious Great Danes go on to live happy, balanced lives once their triggers are understood and addressed.

What is the best calming product for a Great Dane?

The best product depends on your dog's specific triggers. For noise phobias, the ThunderShirt is often the first choice. For dogs who need a secure resting place, a large bolster bed like the K&H Calming Bed can make a big difference. For everyday stress, calming chews with L-theanine or melatonin can help take the edge off. Many owners use a combination of products for the best results.

How much exercise does an anxious Great Dane need?

Most adult Great Danes need at least 60 minutes of exercise daily, split into two or three sessions. However, mental stimulation is just as important as physical activity. Puzzle toys, training sessions, and nose work games can tire a Dane out more effectively than a long walk. Avoid over-exercising growing puppies to protect their joints.

Should I crate my anxious Great Dane?

Crating can be helpful for some anxious Danes, but only if the crate is introduced properly and used as a positive space — never as punishment. The crate must be large enough for your Dane to stand, turn around, and lie down comfortably. If your dog is destructive or panics in a crate, a different safe space (like a gated room or a corner with a bed) may be a better option.

Conclusion

Living with an anxious Great Dane can be challenging, but it's important to remember that your dog isn't being "bad" — they're struggling with a genuine emotional condition. With patience, consistency, and the right tools, you can help your gentle giant feel safer and more secure.

Start by identifying your dog's specific triggers, then build a management plan that includes appropriate exercise, training, a safe space, and supportive products like the ThunderShirt or a calming bed. If the anxiety is severe, don't hesitate to consult your veterinarian.

Your Great Dane has given you their loyalty and love. With the right approach, you can give them the peace of mind they deserve.
